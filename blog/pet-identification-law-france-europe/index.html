Pet Identification Laws in France and Europe 2026: Everything You Need to Know

PetNudge Team March 2, 2026 10 min read
Home/Blog/Pet identification laws

Across France and the European Union, identifying your pet is not merely a recommendation -- it is a legal obligation. Whether you own a dog, a cat, or a ferret, understanding the rules surrounding pet identification is essential for staying compliant with the law and, above all, for keeping your companion safe.

This guide provides a comprehensive overview of French and European legislation in force in 2026, the steps you need to take, the penalties for non-compliance, and how complementary solutions like NFC tags can strengthen your pet's protection.

French Pet Identification Law: What You Need to Know

In France, microchipping has been mandatory for dogs since 1999 and for cats since 2012. This requirement is enshrined in the French Rural Code (articles L. 212-10 and following). Every dog must be identified before the age of 4 months, and every cat before 7 months, or prior to any transfer of ownership -- whether through sale or gift.

Identification is carried out by implanting a microchip (transponder) under the animal's skin, typically in the neck area. The chip contains a unique 15-digit number compliant with the ISO 11784/11785 standard. Tattooing, once the primary method, has become marginal and is no longer accepted as the sole means of identification for international travel.

Which Animals Are Covered?

  • Dogs: mandatory identification before 4 months of age or before any transfer
  • Cats: mandatory identification before 7 months of age or before any transfer
  • Ferrets: mandatory identification for international travel, recommended domestically
  • Exotic pets (NAC): certain species are subject to specific requirements
Good to Know

Since January 1, 2024, French law also requires municipalities to sterilize stray cats. Identification plays a central role in distinguishing between a stray cat and an identified cat that has simply wandered off.

The I-CAD Database: France's National Identification Registry

In France, all pet identifications are centralized in the I-CAD (Identification des Carnivores Domestiques) national database, managed under the authority of the Ministry of Agriculture. This database is the official register linking each microchip number to the owner's contact details.

When your veterinarian microchips your pet, the information is recorded in I-CAD. It is absolutely essential to keep your details up to date in this database. If you move house or change phone numbers, you must update your information on the official i-cad.fr website. An animal whose owner's contact details are outdated has significantly lower chances of being reunited if lost.

Information Recorded in I-CAD

  • Animal's identification number (microchip or tattoo)
  • Name, breed, sex, color, and date of birth
  • Owner's full name, address, and phone number
  • Identifying veterinarian's details
  • Animal's status (at home, lost, stolen, deceased, transferred)

The EU Pet Passport

If you travel with your pet within the European Union, an additional document is required: the European pet passport. This official document, issued by an authorized veterinarian, has been mandatory under EU Regulation No. 576/2013.

The EU pet passport certifies that your animal is microchipped and that vaccinations are up to date, particularly the rabies vaccine. It also contains a detailed description of the animal and the owner's contact information. This document is recognized across all 27 EU member states as well as certain associated countries (Norway, Switzerland, Liechtenstein, and Iceland).

Requirements for Traveling in Europe with Your Pet

  • Microchip: mandatory (tattoos alone have not been accepted since 2011)
  • Rabies vaccination: up to date, administered at least 21 days before departure
  • EU pet passport: issued and completed by an authorized veterinarian
  • Maximum of 5 pets per person (with specific exceptions)

European Regulations in 2026: Recent Developments

The year 2026 brings several significant changes to European pet identification regulations. The European Commission has been working since 2023 on strengthening the harmonization of identification rules at the EU level.

Among the key developments is the reinforcement of interoperability between national databases. The goal is to enable a veterinarian or competent authority in any EU country to quickly access the records of an animal identified in another member state. The Europetnet platform plays an increasingly important role in this interconnection.

Additionally, several member states have strengthened their requirements for cat identification, following France's lead. In 2026, Belgium, the Netherlands, Germany, and Italy all now mandate electronic identification for domestic cats.

Penalties for Non-Compliance

Failing to identify your pet carries real consequences. In France, the absence of identification constitutes a class 4 offense, punishable by a fine of up to 750 euros. In practice, standard fines are typically around 135 euros.

Beyond the fine, an unidentified animal found wandering is considered an ownerless animal. It will be taken to the pound, and after a holding period of 8 working days, may be put up for adoption or, in the worst case, euthanized. An identified animal, on the other hand, allows the authorities to contact its owner immediately.

  • Fine: up to 750 euros (class 4 offense) in France
  • Sale or gift without identification: prohibited and subject to sanctions
  • Animal at the pound: an unidentified animal cannot be automatically returned to its owner
  • Insurance: some pet insurance policies require identification as a prerequisite for coverage
Important

In the event of a veterinary or police check, you must be able to prove that your animal is identified. Always keep a copy of your I-CAD identification card or EU pet passport accessible.

How NFC Tags Complement Legal Identification

The microchip is the legal foundation of pet identification, but it has one significant limitation: it can only be read with a professional reader -- at a veterinary clinic, animal shelter, or by law enforcement. If a kind stranger finds your pet on the street, they have no way to read the chip.

This is where PetNudge NFC tags serve as an indispensable complement. Unlike the subcutaneous chip, an NFC tag can be scanned by any smartphone, with no app required. The finder simply holds their phone near the tag and instantly accesses your pet's profile: name, photo, owner's contact details, medical information, allergies, and current treatments.

  • Accessibility: anyone can scan an NFC tag, not just professionals
  • Speed: immediate contact with the owner, no need to visit a vet first
  • Rich information: medical data, allergies, treatments, vaccinations
  • Real-time updates: modify information anytime from the PetNudge app
  • No app needed: scanning works natively on all modern smartphones

How to Ensure Your Pet Is Compliant in 2026

If your pet is not yet identified, or if you are unsure of your status, here are the steps to follow:

  1. Book an appointment with your veterinarian for microchip implantation. The procedure is quick, painless, and requires no anesthesia.
  2. Verify your I-CAD registration on the i-cad.fr website. Make sure all your contact details are correct and up to date.
  3. Request an EU pet passport from your vet if you plan to travel with your animal.
  4. Add a PetNudge NFC tag for an extra layer of protection that is accessible to everyone.

Summary

Pet identification is both a legal obligation and an act of protection. In France, microchipping and I-CAD registration are mandatory. Across Europe, the EU pet passport is essential for travel. Penalties for non-compliance can reach 750 euros, and more importantly, an unidentified pet is far less likely to be found and returned.
